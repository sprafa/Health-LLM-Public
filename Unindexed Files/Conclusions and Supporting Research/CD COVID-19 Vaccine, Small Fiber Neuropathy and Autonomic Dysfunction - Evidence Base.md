# COVID-19 Vaccine, Small Fiber Neuropathy & Autonomic Dysfunction: Evidence Base

## Summary

There is a growing body of peer-reviewed evidence — mostly case series and retrospective studies, not large RCTs — linking COVID-19 vaccination to the development of small fiber neuropathy (SFN) and autonomic dysfunction. The connection is biologically plausible (immune-mediated mechanism via molecular mimicry), clinically documented, and recognised by multiple academic centres. However, causality remains debated, the absolute incidence is very low, and the evidence quality is mostly Level 3-4 (case series, case-control).

---

## 1. Direct Evidence: SFN After COVID-19 Vaccination

### Waqar Waheed et al. (2021) — First biopsy-proven case

**Source:** Muscle & Nerve, 2021; 64(1):E1-E2. PMC8250971.

**What it shows:** First published case of biopsy-confirmed SFN following COVID-19 vaccination (Pfizer). Patient developed symptoms shortly after vaccination. SARS-CoV-2 antibody profile confirmed post-vaccination state and ruled out prior asymptomatic infection. All other SFN causes excluded.

**Level of evidence:** Single case report.

**Probability of causal link (this case):** ~70%, confidence 50%. Temporal association + exclusion of alternatives, but n=1.

---

### Rigshospitalet Copenhagen Case Series (2025)

**Source:** Journal of the Neurological Sciences, 2025. ScienceDirect. (Pub date ~May 2025)

**What it shows:** 16 patients referred with suspected SFN after COVID-19 vaccination (symptom onset within 30 days). 9 of 16 fulfilled diagnostic criteria for SFN. 88% had burning/tingling sensation, typically length-dependent. Vaccines involved: Pfizer, Moderna, AstraZeneca.

**Level of evidence:** Prospective case series (Level 4).

**Key quote from authors:** "Defining the causality is arduous, but temporality, analogies with other vaccinations and/or infections support our hypothesis."

---

### Donadio et al. (2024) — Prospective case-control, Bologna

**Source:** European Journal of Neurology (PubMed 39526678), published Nov 2024.

**What it shows:** Prospective case-control study comparing SFN after COVID-19 infection vs. vaccination using skin biopsy with confocal microscopy. Both groups showed reduced intraepidermal nerve fiber density (IENFD) compared to controls. Cutoff values established: 9.6/mm for lower leg (80% sensitivity, 96% specificity), 12.8/mm for thigh (77% sensitivity, 100% specificity).

**Level of evidence:** Prospective case-control (Level 3). This is one of the stronger studies methodologically.

---

### Bandinelli et al. (2024) — Florence, SFN + arthritis post-vaccine

**Source:** International Journal of Molecular Sciences, 2024. PMC11355276.

**What it shows:** 19 patients with post-COVID/post-vaccine undifferentiated arthritis and new-onset lower limb paresthesia were skin-biopsied. Confirmed SFN in a subset. Proposed molecular mimicry between spike protein and human proteins as mechanism.

**Level of evidence:** Retrospective case series (Level 4).

## 2. Broader PNS Complications of COVID-19 Vaccination (Context)

### BMC Neurology Systematic Review (2023)

**Source:** BMC Neurology, 2023. Springer. (link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12883-023-03486-y)

**What it shows:** Pooled analysis of 258 cases of neuromuscular disease following COVID-19 vaccination: 171 Guillain-Barré syndrome, 40 Parsonage-Turner syndrome, 22 myasthenia gravis, 19 facial nerve palsy, 5 SFN. Median time from vaccine to symptom onset was <2 weeks in all groups.

**Level of evidence:** Systematic review of case reports/series (Level 3-4).

---

### Journal of Integrative Neuroscience Review (2025)

**Source:** JIN, March 2025. (imrpress.com/journal/JIN/24/3/10.31083/JIN26632)

**What it shows:** Comprehensive review of PNS complications after COVID-19 vaccination. Lists SFN as a recognised complication alongside GBS, PTS, cranial mononeuropathies, and MG. States: "Diagnosis of small fiber neuropathy in the setting of COVID-19 vaccination should be suspected in patients with dysesthesia, dysautonomia, and lower extremity paresthesia."

**Level of evidence:** Narrative review.

## 3. The FND / Autoimmune Overlap — The Nakane Study

### Nagata, Nakane et al. (2023) — gAChR antibodies in FND patients

**Source:** Frontiers in Neurology, 2023; 14:1137958. PMC9968745.

**What it shows:** 59 patients diagnosed with Functional Neurological Symptom Disorder (FNSD/conversion disorder) were tested for ganglionic acetylcholine receptor (gAChR) antibodies. 27% tested positive. Antibody-positive patients had significantly higher rates of cardiovascular autonomic dysfunction.

**Why this matters for this case:** The patient has been diagnosed with FND by a London neurologist. If 27% of FND patients actually have detectable autoantibodies against autonomic ganglia, some proportion of "FND" diagnoses may be misclassified autoimmune autonomic ganglionopathy (AAG).

**Level of evidence:** Cross-sectional study (Level 3). Single-centre (Kagoshima, Japan). Uses LIPS assay rather than the standard radioimmunoprecipitation used at Mayo Clinic, which may affect comparability.

**Caveats:** The LIPS assay may have different sensitivity/specificity than the standard RIP assay. The 27% positivity rate is surprisingly high and has not been independently replicated. The study does not prove these patients were misdiagnosed — it raises the question.

**Probability that this finding generalises:** ~40%, confidence 35%. Provocative but unreplicated.

## 4. Proposed Mechanism

The dominant hypothesis across these studies is **molecular mimicry**: the spike protein (whether from infection or vaccination) shares structural similarities with human neural proteins. The immune response generated against the spike protein cross-reacts with components of the peripheral nervous system, particularly small nerve fibers and autonomic ganglia.

### Specific proposed targets:

- **Small unmyelinated C-fibers** (sensory and autonomic) — leading to SFN
- **Ganglionic nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (α3 subunit)** — leading to AAG
- **FGFR3 and TS-HDS** — other neuropathy-associated antibodies found in some post-COVID/vaccine SFN patients

### This is mechanistically plausible given:

- Known precedent of post-vaccination GBS (influenza vaccines)
- Animal models showing immunization against neuronal AChR produces autonomic failure (Lennon et al., J Clin Invest 2003)
- The autonomic nervous system relying on AChR-mediated ganglionic transmission, which is vulnerable to antibody-mediated disruption

**Source for mechanism:** Vernino et al., Autonomic Neuroscience 2009 (PMC2677210); Nakane et al., Expert Rev Neurother 2018.

## 6. Important Caveats

**Absolute incidence is very low.** Post-vaccine neuropathies are reported at 0.01-0.13% of vaccinated individuals. Most vaccinated people have no neurological complications.

**Correlation ≠ causation.** Temporal association after vaccination does not prove the vaccine caused the SFN. Some cases may be coincidental.

**Evidence quality is mostly Level 3-4.** No randomised controlled trials exist (nor would they be ethical for this question). The strongest evidence comes from case-control studies with biopsy confirmation.

**The Nakane/FND study is unreplicated.** The 27% gAChR positivity in FND patients is a single study from one Japanese centre. It needs independent replication before being treated as established.

**This is not anti-vaccine evidence.** The risk-benefit ratio of COVID-19 vaccination remains strongly favourable. These are rare adverse events in a small subset of individuals, possibly with pre-existing immune susceptibility.
